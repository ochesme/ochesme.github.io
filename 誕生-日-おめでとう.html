25. お守り means to protect. It is said to provide various forms of luck or protection.


The kanji is translated as omamori, in English ‘amulet’ or ‘talisman’. The kanji literally means “to guide or protect,” from the word mamori, means protection. Omamori are Japanese amulets commonly sold and can be obtained at Shinto shrines and Buddhist temples. Anyone can purchase them, regardless of their religion affiliation. They are meant to be put on your phone, purse, wallet, home wall, pocket, bags, in cars, or the like, to ward off bad luck and provide various forms of luck or protection.

It is originally made from paper or wood with prayers written on it, the amulets covering is usually made of brocade silk bag. Prayers written on it are supposed to bring good luck to the bearer on particular occasions, tasks or ordeals. There are different types of omamori, below is a list of the most common types of omamori:

kai-un: general good fortune yakuyoke: warding off evil katsumori: for success gakugyou jouju: academic success shoubai hanjou: for money and business shiawase: for happiness kenkou: health anzan: save childbirth byouki heiyu: recovering from sickness ryokou: safe travelling koutsu anzen: traffic safety enmusubi: for love / finding a partner
