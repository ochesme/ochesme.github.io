Answer:

If you were to read through the biblical gospels, you would find that Jesus shares “the honours due to God.” No one, in the whole Bible, shares the honours due to God. Only Jesus. And yet, throughout that gospel, we read of Jesus being the object of worship (2:2, 11; 8:2; 9:18; 14:33; 15:25; 20:20; 28:9, 17).[2] Or consider, also, when Jesus walked on water, calmed the sea, and saved Peter (His disciple) from drowning, Matthew writes “Then those who were in the boat came and worshiped Him, saying, ‘Truly You are the Son of God’” (14:33). And when Jesus asked the disciples who they believed He was, Simon Peter confessed “You are the Christ, the Son of the living God” (16:16). And after His bodily resurrection, the disciples gathered on a mount in Galilee and “when they saw Him, they worshiped Him” (28:17). These are a few examples as to how Jesus shared the honours due to God.

In regards to healing, there is a verse famous misused by many Christians.

1 Peter 2:24He personally carried our sins in his body on the cross so that we can be dead to sin and live for what is right. By his wounds you are healed.

Christians often use this verse as an encouragement or prayer when sick. However, "By his wounds you are healed" doesn't refer to diseases or sickness. The correct way to read it is to add the following verse, 1 Peter 2:24-25.

1 Peter 2:25Once you were like sheep who wandered away. But now you have turned to your Shepherd, the Guardian of your souls.

19. why did Jesus ask those whom he Healed to never to tell anyone what he did? ​


JESUS' POWER OF HEALING

Answer:

1. Jesus had no intention of discouraging people from coming to hear Him proclaim the Gospel.

He was sent for the aim of preaching and instructing. "The Spirit of the Lord is upon Me because He has anointed Me to preach the gospel," He said of Himself, quoting the prophet Isaiah (Luke 4:18).

So why did He warn them not to tell? Because publicizing His ability to heal would jeopardize His teaching work.

2. The hoards of people made it impossible for Jesus and His apostles to receive even normal—necessary—rest.

Jesus could scarcely escape away for a moment's respite. The fact that He could sleep on a boat during a furious storm while the others were terrified of drowning may reflect how exhausted He was at times. "Now, once they had left the crowd, they put Him in the boat as He was." And He was accompanied by several little vessels. And then a huge breeze blew up, and the waves pounded into the boat, filling it to the brim. But He was sleeping on a cushion in the stern. And they awakened Him and asked, 'Teacher, do You not care that we are dying?' ’(Mark 4:36-38). Not only were He and the Apostles looking for a secluded spot to slumber, but it appears that even then they had limited options. "Then the apostles gathered around Jesus and told Him everything, both what they had done and what they had taught." And He said to them, 'Come away by yourself to a lonely spot and rest a bit,' since there were so many people coming and going that they didn't even have time to eat with Him." (Mark 6:30–31).

It appears that the dissemination of knowledge about His recovery hampered His principal goal––preaching the Gospel. Any healing He did for the ill was a little and temporary benefit in comparison to the long-term worth of the Divine plan He was presenting. He healed so that some might believe and obey His teachings and become a part of the eternal Kingdom He would establish on earth one day with Him.

22. Was bin laden really responsible for 9/11


Answer:

No event in recent times has produced as many explanations as the 11th September attacks five years ago. Within the space of an hour, al Qaeda inflicted more direct damage on the US than the Soviet Union had done throughout the cold war, a cataclysm seen by more people than any other event in history. Yet it took only 19 men armed with small knives to destroy the World Trade Centre, demolish a wing of the Pentagon and kill 3,000 people. This mismatch has led some—especially in the Muslim world—to seek a deus ex machina to explain what otherwise appears inexplicable. The usual suspects have been assembled on 9/11’s grassy knoll: the Jews were behind the attacks; the US government engineered them; the “Cheney-Bush energy junta” planned them so that they could grab the oil fields of central Asia, and so on.

Osama bin Laden himself claims that al Qaeda was solely responsible for 9/11. In 2004, he released a video in which he explained his dealings with lead hijacker Mohammed Atta. After the largest criminal investigation in history, the US government’s 9/11 commission also concluded that al Qaeda was solely responsible for the attacks.

Attributing the sole responsibility for 9/11 to al Qaeda then brings us to the larger question: what caused al Qaeda to launch the attacks? Explanations for the attacks can be sorted into two categories—the seemingly plausible but flawed, and the more credible.
